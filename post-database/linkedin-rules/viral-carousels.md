# Viral Carousels & Infographics Rules

What makes carousel and infographic posts perform on LinkedIn. These formats get the highest save rates and longest shelf life.

---

## Why Carousels Work

- **Swipe mechanic** = time on post goes up = algorithm pushes it harder
- **Highest save rate** of any format — people bookmark them as references
- **Repurposable** — one carousel can become 5 standalone posts later
- **Educational format** — perfect for how-tos, frameworks, and breakdowns
- **Shareability** — teams share carousels in Slack/email more than text posts

---

## Carousel Formats That Work

### Tier 1 — Proven

| Format | Structure | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Step-by-step guide** | Slide 1: hook. Slides 2-8: one step per slide. Last slide: CTA | Teaching a process (how to set up outbound, how to use Clay) |
| **Framework breakdown** | Slide 1: framework name. Slides 2-6: one pillar per slide with explanation. Last slide: summary | Sharing your methodology |
| **Myth vs reality** | Each slide: one myth on left, reality on right | Busting misconceptions in your industry |
| **Before → after** | Slide 1: the problem. Middle slides: the transformation. Last slide: the result | Showing a client result or process improvement |

### Tier 2 — Solid

| Format | Structure | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| **Listicle** | "7 tools I use for [X]" — one tool per slide | Tool roundups, resource lists |
| **Data breakdown** | Charts, numbers, metrics — one insight per slide | When you have real data to share |
| **Teardown** | Analyze someone's email/landing page/funnel slide by slide | Educational + social proof if you tag the person |
| **Comparison** | Tool A vs Tool B, side by side per slide | When your audience is choosing between options |

## Carousel Design Rules

### Structure
1. **Slide 1 is the hook.** It must work as a standalone image in the feed. Bold text, clear question or promise.
2. **One idea per slide.** Never cram two points on one slide.
3. **6-10 slides total.** Under 6 feels thin. Over 10 loses attention.
4. **Last slide = CTA.** "Save this for later" / "Follow for more [topic]" / "DM me [word] for the template"
5. **Consistent visual style.** Same colors, fonts, layout across all slides.

### Visual
1. **Dark background, light text** OR **light background, dark text.** High contrast = readable on mobile.
2. **Font size: 24pt minimum.** Anything smaller is unreadable on phone.
3. **Max 30 words per slide.** Less is more.
4. **Use icons/illustrations sparingly.** One per slide max. They support the text, not replace it.
5. **No stock photos.** Use screenshots, diagrams, or clean illustrations.
6. **Brand it subtly.** Your name/handle on every slide, small, corner. Not a giant logo.

### Copy
1. **Headlines on every slide.** Bold, punchy, scannable.
2. **Write for skimmers.** Someone swiping fast should get the gist from headlines alone.
3. **Use numbers.** "Step 1, Step 2" or "Tool #3" — gives structure and progress.
4. **End with a question.** The last slide should make people want to comment.

## Infographic Rules

Infographics are single-image posts (not swipeable). Different rules:

1. **Vertical format.** LinkedIn crops horizontal images. Go tall.
2. **One clear visual hierarchy.** Title → key insight → supporting data → source.
3. **3-5 data points max.** More than that and nothing stands out.
4. **Use comparison or ranking.** "Top 5 tools by [metric]" performs better than random stats.
5. **Always cite the source.** "Source: G2, 2026" adds credibility.
6. **Pair with a text post.** The infographic catches the eye, the caption gives context.

## What Makes a Carousel Viral

| Factor | Impact |
|---|---|
| **Slide 1 hook** | 80% of performance is determined by the first slide |
| **Practical value** | "I can use this today" = save + share |
| **Clean design** | Ugly carousels get swiped past regardless of content |
| **Specificity** | "How to write cold emails" < "How we booked 47 meetings with a 3-line email" |
| **CTA on last slide** | "Save this" drives the save rate, which boosts algorithm |

### From Charles Tenot's 50 posts (deep analysis 2026-04-01)

**Finding: Charles uses zero carousels.** All 50 posts are text-only. His educational posts (avg 243 likes) underperform his other types. Carousels could be the unlock.

**Why his educational posts underperform:**

His educational posts avg 243 likes vs 452 for hot takes and 450 for stories. Why? Educational text posts are harder to consume on mobile — long lists, bullet points, frameworks all get compressed in the LinkedIn feed. The same content as a carousel would:
- Force one idea per swipe (easier to digest)
- Increase time-on-post (algorithm boost)
- Drive saves (highest save-rate format)
- Make the content shareable in Slack/email

**His top posts that should have been carousels:**

| Post | Likes | Why it'd work better as a carousel |
|---|---|---|
| "10X LTV/CAC acquisition strategy breakdown" | 219 | Multi-step strategy = one step per slide |
| "Buyers who talk to Sales convert 4X more" | 217 | PLG sales framework = perfect for slide-by-slide |
| "We rebuilt the SDR playbook from scratch" | 294 | Old vs new playbook = before/after carousel |
| "4 hard truths about SaaS GTM" | 164 | 4 truths = 4 slides. Clean. |
| "Spammy cold emails are dead — evolution of outbound over 7 years" | 191 | Timeline = perfect carousel format |
| "How we boost email deliverability by 40%" | 174 | Step-by-step process = classic carousel |
| "4 reasons we shouldn't have bought Claap" | 178 | Pros/cons = myth vs reality carousel |

**Repurposing opportunity:**

These 7 posts averaged 205 likes as text. As carousels, based on industry benchmarks for carousel vs text performance, they could realistically hit 300-400+ likes AND generate 3-5x more saves. That's 7 ready-made carousels waiting to be designed.

**Carousel rules derived from his content style:**

1. **Lead with a number.** His best educational hooks all use numbers (10X, 4X, 40%, 4 truths). Carousel slide 1 should always have a number.
2. **One framework per carousel.** His educational posts that try to cover too much (SDR playbook) underperform. Keep it to one clear framework.
3. **"We did X, here's what happened" structure.** His audience responds to proof, not theory. Every carousel should show real results.
4. **End with the honest part.** His best posts all include "what didn't work." Last slide before CTA should be the honest caveat.
