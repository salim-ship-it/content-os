# Commenting Rules

How to write comments that get impressions, build relationships, and grow your audience. Every rule here comes from analyzing which comments on winning posts got the most likes and replies.

---

## Why Commenting Matters

- A great comment on a viral post can get you more impressions than your own post
- Creators notice and remember people who leave smart comments
- LinkedIn's algorithm shows your comment to your followers' feeds
- It's the fastest way to get in front of a new audience without posting

---

## Comment Patterns That Work

### Tier 1 — High impression comments

| Pattern | Example | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| **Add a missing angle** | "This is spot on. One thing I'd add: [insight the post missed]" | Shows you thought deeper than the post. Creator often replies. |
| **Share your own data** | "We tested this at VectorLabs — our reply rate went from 2% to 8% when we added [X]" | Specific numbers = credibility. People click your profile. |
| **Respectful disagreement** | "I see this differently. In my experience [counter-example]..." | Tension drives replies. Other people pile on both sides. |
| **Ask a sharp question** | "Curious — did this work the same way for enterprise vs SMB?" | Forces the creator to reply. Shows expertise in the question itself. |

### Tier 2 — Solid comments

| Pattern | Example | Why it works |
|---|---|---|
| **Expand with a story** | "This reminds me of when we [1-2 sentence story]" | Adds a human element. People relate to stories. |
| **Tag someone relevant** | "This is exactly what @[name] was saying last week about [topic]" | Pulls another person into the conversation. Doubles reach. |
| **Summarize + add** | "Key takeaway: [one line]. I'd also add [one line]." | Shows you actually read the post. Easy to like. |

### Tier 3 — Avoid these

| Anti-pattern | Why it kills your reach |
|---|---|
| "Great post!" / "Love this!" | Zero value. LinkedIn buries it. |
| "Agree 100%" | Nothing to engage with. |
| Long paragraphs with no structure | Nobody reads a wall of text in comments. |
| Self-promotion with no connection | "Check out my tool that does [X]" — spam. |
| Emoji-only reactions | Not even worth the click. |

## Commenting Strategy

### Daily routine (10 min)
1. Open LinkedIn
2. Find 5-10 posts from creators in `sources.md`
3. Leave one high-quality comment on each
4. Prioritize posts that are 0-2 hours old (early comments get more visibility)

### Rules
- **2-4 sentences max** — short, punchy, value-packed
- **Lead with your take, not agreement** — "Here's what I'd add" > "I agree"
- **One idea per comment** — don't try to say everything
- **Reply to replies** — if someone responds to your comment, keep the thread going
- **Comment on posts from people slightly bigger than you** — their audience becomes your audience

### From Charles Tenot's 50 posts (deep analysis 2026-04-01)

**Comment volume by post type:**

| Post type | Avg comments | Highest | What drives comments |
|---|---|---|---|
| Case studies | 79 | 159 | People share their own version ("here's what worked for me") |
| Announcements | 67 | 184 | Congratulations + questions about the news |
| Hot takes | 62 | 102 | Debate — people take sides |
| Transparency | 52 | 74 | "How did you do that?" questions |
| Stories | 47 | 78 | Emotional responses + "me too" sharing |
| Educational | 48 | 70 | Follow-up questions + people adding their own tips |
| Insightful | 54 | 92 | Technical discussion, especially on AI/tool posts |

**Key findings:**

1. **Case studies drive the deepest comment threads.** His #1 post (1310 likes, 159 comments) was a cold email teardown. People didn't just comment — they shared their own best emails. Lesson: if you want comments, show YOUR real example, and people will show theirs.

2. **Hot takes get the highest comment-to-like ratio.** "6x7 > 8x5" got 102 comments on 406 likes (25% ratio). Compare to stories: 844 likes but only 76 comments (9% ratio). Lesson: if you want comments specifically, write hot takes. If you want reach, write stories.

3. **The posts with 100+ comments all share one trait:** they contain a built-in question or tension that people MUST respond to. "Am I wrong?" "What do you think?" "Have you experienced this?" It's never a clean conclusion — there's always an open loop.

4. **Name-dropping drives replies from the named person.** Posts mentioning Guillaume Moubeche, Tom Greenwood, Pierre Bruneau — the named person comments, which pulls their audience in. Lesson: tag real people in your comments too.

5. **MCP/AI tool posts get disproportionate comments from technical people.** His CTO/MCP post (170 likes, 92 comments) has the second-highest comment ratio in the entire dataset. Technical content attracts builders who love discussing details.

**How to comment on posts like these:**
- On case studies → share your own real example with numbers
- On hot takes → pick a side and explain why with one personal experience
- On transparency posts → ask a specific follow-up ("How did you handle X when Y happened?")
- On announcements → add context the post didn't ("This matters because...")
- On AI/tool posts → share how you're using the same tool differently
